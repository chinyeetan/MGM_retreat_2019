Introduction

  • Microbiome modulation is an attractive method to promote health and abrogate disease (Schmidt, Raes, and Bork 2018). Members of the host microbiota regulate host immunity (Blander et al. 2017), illuminating an entry point for the treatment of autoimmune and inflammatory diseases.
  • Yet it is non-trivial to pinpoint species that causally impact host health from a laundry list of taxa associated with disease (Fischbach 2018).
  • Mice stably colonized with human microbiota (HMb) are less susceptible to chemically induced colitis than those colonized with mouse microbiota (MMb).
  • A technique we use in the Surana lab: Microbe-phenotype triangulation (Surana and Kasper 2017) identified a hitherto unknown taxon of bacteria abundant in HMb but absent in MMb that was associated with attenuated disease in a colitis model.
    Subsequent characterization led to the discovery of a new species, Clostridium immunis.

Figure 1: The first image of C. immunis captured by Transmission Electron Microscopy.

Approach

  • Extending the immunomodulatory potential of C. immunis to other disease models.
  • Elucidating the ‘where’ and ‘when’ of C. immunis colonization to direct the search for mechanistic clues.
  • Identifying the changes in intestinal immune cell population induced by C. immunis.
  • Genetic manipulation of C. immunis to identify bacterial bioactive products which mediate immunomodulation.

Results

Oral administration of C. immunis mitigates disease severity in two distinct mouse models of inflammatory disease

Figure 2: Oral gavage of C. immunis (A) reduces lethality in DSS colitis, and (B) improves clinical severity in experimental autoimmune encephalitis. n=5 mice per group (DSS colitis); n=10 mice per group (EAE); *=p<0.05


C. immunis is localized to the cecum and proximal-middle colon;
Presence of C. immunis in the naïve mouse gut is short-lived

Figure 3: (A) C. immunis is localized to the cecum and proximal-mid colon in colonized mice at steady-state (HMb); n=4 mice (B) C. immunis naïve MMb mice do not retain C. immunis beyond 24 hours after single oral gavage; n=9 mice

Oral administration of a C. immunis enriched culture decreases the abundance of colonic Group 3 innate lymphoid cells (ILC3)

Figure 4: Colonic group 3 innate lymphoid cells were decreased in mice gavaged with a culture enriched for C. immunis (HMb cx) compared to a culture without C. immunis (MMb cx); n=6-7 mice per group. **=p<0.005

Ongoing work

Bacterial end: Comparative genomics and genetic manipulation of C. immunis to identify bacterial products that modulate host immunity

Figure 5: Whole genome comparisons between C. immunis and closely related species will identify candidate genes that confer disease protection. These genes will be targeted by CRISPR in genetically engineered C. immunis

Host end: Unravelling changes to host immunological status mediated by C. immunis

  • I am currently generating ILC3 knockout mice to confirm and study the mechanism of C. immunis modulation of the host colonic ILC3 population.

Phenotype and Impact:
Oral administration of C. immunis to mice ameliorates inflammatory disease

Mechanism:
(a) Colonization by C. immunis is not required for its protective effect in disease
b) Disease protection possibly occurs via locally downregulating the intestinal group 3 innate lymphoid cell (ILC3) population

Ongoing work:
a) Identifying bioactive products of C. immunis
b) Characterizing changes to host immune cells induced by C. immunis
